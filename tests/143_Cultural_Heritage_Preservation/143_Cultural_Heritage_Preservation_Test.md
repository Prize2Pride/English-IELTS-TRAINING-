**The Power of Preserving Cultural Heritage**

Historical landmarks, living heritage and natural sites enrich our daily lives in countless ways, whether we experience them directly or through the medium of a connected device. Cultural diversity and creativity are natural drivers of innovation. In many ways, artists, creators and performers help us change our perspective on the world and rethink our environment. These are precious assets to respond to current global challenges, from the climate crisis to the COVID-19 pandemic.

The notion of culture has greatly evolved over the last 75 years. UNESCO’s actions over the past decades bear witness to the many ways in which humanity tried to understand how culture can strengthen the sense of who we are – from the awareness of the necessity to protect heritage from destruction at the end of World War II, to the launch of international campaigns to safeguard World Heritage sites and the concept of living and intangible heritage, a focus on creative economy and the need to sustain cultural jobs and livelihoods. Our relationship with culture has deeply evolved over the last century. If we look into the past, we might be better prepared to tackle further changes ahead.

In the course of an unprecedented safeguarding campaign to save the temples of Egypt, UNESCO demonstrated that humanity does not have to sacrifice the past to thrive in the present – quite the opposite. Monuments of outstanding universal value help us understand who we are and also represent massive opportunities for development. Two millennia after a Greek author and scientist drew the famous list of the world’s seven wonders, the very notion of World Heritage came to life. The race against time began in 1964, when experts from 50 nations started working together under the coordination of UNESCO in one of the greatest challenges of archaeological engineering in history. The entire site was carefully cut into large blocks, dismantled, lifted and reassembled in a new location 65 metres higher and 200 metres back from the river, preserving it for future generations.

Today, the four majestic statues that guard the entrance to the great temple stare at the river and the rising sun every day. As they did 3,000 years ago. The success of the international cooperation to save Abu Simbel raised awareness about the fact that all over the world there are places of outstanding universal value. Just like the Nile valley temples, they must be protected from many threats such as armed conflict, deliberate destruction, economic pressure, natural disasters and climate change. The World Heritage Convention was adopted in 1972 as the most important global instrument to establish this notion, bringing all nations together in the pursuit of the preservation of the World’s Natural and Cultural Heritage. With its 194 signatory Member States, it is today one of the world’s most ratified conventions.

Launched only a few years after the Nubian temples initiative, the safeguarding campaign for Venice was a response to various challenges including the rising waters and the explosion of global tourism. Yet, the breath-taking beauty that inspired countless painters, writers and artists over the centuries remains fragile and at risk of being lost forever. Like the Abu Simbel temples, the city’s survival is threatened by rising water levels. The inexorable increase in sea level has caused flooding to become a regular occurrence. Humidity and microorganisms are eating away the long wooden piles that early dwellers drove deep into the muddy ground of the lagoon to build the first foundations of Venice, 1,600 years ago.

After 1966, the year of the worst flooding in Venice’s history, UNESCO and the Italian Government launched a major campaign to save the city. An ambitious project involving giant mobile flood gates was undertaken to temporarily isolate the lagoon from the high tides and protect the lowest areas from flooding. Thirty years later there is unanimous agreement on the successful results both of the technical achievements and international cooperation. But Venice still needs attentive care, and its continued survival calls for unflagging vigilance. The city remains threatened on several fronts – mass tourism, the potential damage of subsequent urban development and the steady stream of giant cruise ships crushing its brittle foundations.

International mobilization and pressure around the status of Venice led to the Italian Government’s decision in 2021 to ban large ships from the city centre, as a necessary step to protect the environmental, landscape, artistic and cultural integrity of Venice. This decision came a few days after UNESCO announced its intention to inscribe the city on its World Heritage in Danger list. Until a permanent big cruise docking place is identified and developed, liners will be permitted to pull up in Marghera, an industrial suburb of Venice. Such decisions illustrate the great complexity of protecting historic cities and cultural heritage urban centres, which in this particular situation called for tailor-made measures and techniques different from those implemented for the safeguarding of the fabled Egyptian temples.

### READING PASSAGE 2

**Digital Heritage: Preserving the Past for the Future**

**A.** The preservation of our collective cultural heritage has entered a new era, thanks to the rapid advancements in digital technology. Traditionally, the safeguarding of historical artifacts and sites was the domain of specialized institutions, with access to these treasures often limited to a privileged few. However, the digital revolution is democratizing access to our shared history. Technologies such as virtual reality (VR), augmented reality (AR), and interactive digital platforms are dismantling the physical barriers of the past, offering immersive and engaging experiences that foster a more profound connection with history. Innovations like 3D modeling and digital archives are not only making heritage more accessible but also providing new methods to protect it from threats like environmental degradation and the ravages of time, ensuring its long-term survival.

**B.** One of the most exciting developments in this field is the use of 3D geospatial technology to create photorealistic digital replicas of real-world environments. Companies like Cesium, in partnership with Google Maps, are providing developers with the tools to build incredibly detailed and immersive digital worlds. By using Photorealistic 3D Tiles, developers can create highly accurate representations of cities and landscapes. This technology, while primarily focused on gaming and simulations, has immense potential for cultural heritage preservation. It allows for the creation of detailed digital twins of historical sites, which can be used for documentation, research, and virtual tourism. These digital replicas can serve as a vital record of a site's current state and can be used to monitor changes over time.

**C.** Artificial intelligence (AI) is also playing a transformative role in art conservation and restoration. The Rijksmuseum in Amsterdam, for instance, has successfully used AI to digitally reconstruct Rembrandt's masterpiece, "The Night Watch." By analyzing historical records and using sophisticated algorithms, the museum was able to recreate missing sections of the painting that were trimmed off in the 18th century. This not only restored the painting to its original composition but also revealed details that had been lost for centuries. The use of multispectral imaging and X-ray fluorescence provides further insights into the artist's techniques and materials, deepening our understanding of the creative process. Furthermore, the museum's open data policy allows researchers and the public to engage with the art in new and interactive ways.

**D.** The application of AI extends beyond paintings to the reconstruction of fragmented artifacts. At MIT's Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL), researchers are developing algorithms that can digitally reassemble broken pottery and other artifacts. By analyzing the shape, texture, and patterns of the fragments, the AI can predict the original form of the object, much like solving a complex jigsaw puzzle. This technology is not only faster and more accurate than manual reconstruction but can also hypothesize about missing pieces, providing archaeologists with invaluable insights. The digital models created through this process can be 3D-printed, allowing for detailed study without risking damage to the original fragile artifacts.

**E.** The world of video games has also unexpectedly contributed to cultural heritage preservation. The popular game series *Assassin's Creed* is renowned for its meticulously researched and historically accurate recreations of cities from various periods. For *Assassin's Creed Unity*, set during the French Revolution, the developers created a stunningly detailed 1:1 scale model of 18th-century Paris, including a faithful reproduction of the Notre Dame Cathedral. After the tragic fire in 2019, the game's 3D models were offered as a valuable reference for the restoration efforts. This demonstrates the potential of gaming technology to serve as an unintentional archive of our built heritage.

**F.** Beyond mere documentation, technology is also being used to preserve and share intangible cultural heritage. The video game *Never Alone* (Kisima Inŋitchuŋa) is a prime example. Developed in collaboration with the Iñupiat people of Alaska, the game immerses players in their traditional myths and folklore. As players navigate the game's world, they unlock documentary videos featuring Iñupiat elders sharing their stories and cultural traditions. This innovative approach to storytelling not only entertains but also educates a global audience about a unique indigenous culture, ensuring that their oral traditions are passed on to future generations in a new and engaging format.

**G.** The convergence of technology and heritage preservation marks a significant shift in how we interact with the past. However, this progress is not without its challenges. There is a risk of oversimplifying complex histories in the pursuit of engaging digital experiences. It is crucial to strike a balance between technological innovation and historical authenticity. Furthermore, meaningful community engagement is vital. By collaborating with historians, archaeologists, and the communities connected to these heritage sites, we can ensure that digital preservation initiatives are not only technologically impressive but also culturally sensitive and historically accurate. In doing so, we can harness the power of technology to build a richer, more inclusive understanding of our shared human story.

**The Future of Our Past: Challenges and Hopes in Heritage Preservation**

The World Monuments Fund (WMF) has identified a host of specific human factors threatening over 200 heritage sites under review for its 2025 World Monuments Watch. The data, collected from nominations by heritage professionals and the public, reveals a complex web of challenges that demand a multifaceted and collaborative approach to preservation.

One of the most significant threats is **climate change**. Extreme weather events, rising sea levels, and more frequent natural disasters are putting immense stress on heritage sites globally. In Sub-Saharan Africa, a staggering 74% of nominated sites highlighted climate hazards as a critical issue. In response, organizations like the WMF have launched initiatives such as the Climate Heritage Initiative to help communities adapt and protect their heritage. This includes projects like the restoration of the Hitis (water fountains) of the Kathmandu Valley in Nepal, which are vital to the local community and threatened by changing rainfall patterns.

**Rapid urbanization** is another major concern, particularly in Asia and the Pacific, where 51% of nominated sites cited it as a primary threat. Unchecked urban development often leads to the demolition of historic buildings and neighborhoods in favor of new construction. Promoting sustainable urban planning that integrates heritage preservation is crucial for creating livable cities that honor their past while embracing the future.

**Overtourism**, or irresponsible tourism, poses a significant threat to many of the world’s most iconic landmarks. Overcrowding, lack of visitor management, and inadequate infrastructure can lead to the physical degradation of sites. In Latin America and the Caribbean, 43% of nominated sites were concerned with the impacts of tourism. The key to mitigating this threat lies in encouraging sustainable tourism practices that respect heritage sites and actively involve local communities. This approach not only helps protect the sites but also ensures that the economic benefits of tourism are shared more equitably.

**Conflict and crises**, both armed and natural, continue to wreak havoc on cultural heritage. The destruction of heritage sites in conflict zones is not only a cultural loss but also a blow to the identity and social cohesion of communities. In the Middle East and North Africa, 40% of nominated sites reported issues related to human conflict. Post-conflict recovery efforts, such as the revitalization of the Mosul Cultural Museum in Iraq, demonstrate the power of heritage in rebuilding communities and fostering peace.

Beyond these external threats, many heritage sites suffer from a **lack of recognition and insufficient funding**. In Europe and North America, 58% of nominated sites cited underfunding as their primary obstacle. The economic value of heritage is often underestimated, but investing in preservation can create jobs, stimulate local economies, and foster a sense of community pride. Highlighting these benefits is crucial for securing the necessary financial support for preservation efforts.

Finally, **limited community engagement** and a lack of local expertise threaten the long-term sustainability of many heritage sites. When local communities are not involved in the preservation process, the sites can become disconnected from the very people who are their most important stewards. Empowering local communities with the training and resources they need to care for their own heritage is essential for ensuring its survival for future generations. The World Monuments Watch, through its collaboration with local nominators, is a testament to the power of community-led preservation.

The challenges facing our shared cultural heritage are immense and varied. However, by understanding the specific threats in different regions and by fostering a collaborative approach that involves governments, non-profit organizations, and, most importantly, local communities, we can hope to protect these irreplaceable treasures for generations to come. The future of our past depends on the actions we take today.

It includes all traces of human existence having a cultural, historical, or archaeological character which have been partially or totally underwater, periodically or continuously, for at least 100 years. This can include everything from ancient shipwrecks and submerged cities to prehistoric landscapes that are now covered by the sea.

One of the most significant types of underwater cultural heritage is, of course, the shipwreck. It is estimated that there are over three million shipwrecks scattered across the world’s oceans, and each one is a unique time capsule, offering a snapshot of a particular moment in history. The cargo, the personal belongings of the crew, and the ship’s structure itself can all provide invaluable information about the past.

However, the preservation of underwater cultural heritage faces a number of significant challenges. The marine environment is extremely harsh, and artifacts can be quickly destroyed by currents, chemical processes, and marine organisms. In addition, many sites are threatened by human activities, such as fishing, dredging, and, of course, looting. The illegal salvage of artifacts from shipwrecks is a major problem, and it is estimated that the black market for underwater antiquities is worth millions of dollars every year.

To address these challenges, a number of international conventions have been established. The most important of these is the 2001 UNESCO Convention on the Protection of the Underwater Cultural Heritage. This convention sets out a legal framework for the protection of submerged sites and promotes international cooperation in this area. It also emphasizes the importance of in situ preservation, which means leaving artifacts on the seabed wherever possible, as this is often the best way to ensure their long-term survival.

Of course, in some cases, it is necessary to excavate and recover artifacts. This is a highly specialized process that requires a team of experts, including marine archaeologists, conservators, and technicians. The use of remotely operated vehicles, or ROVs, has revolutionized underwater archaeology, allowing researchers to explore sites that were previously inaccessible. These robots can be equipped with high-definition cameras, sonar systems, and manipulator arms, enabling them to carry out detailed surveys and even recover small artifacts.

Once artifacts have been recovered, they need to undergo a lengthy and complex process of conservation. This is because objects that have been submerged in water for a long period of time are often very fragile and can disintegrate if they are not treated properly. The conservation process can take many years and involves a variety of techniques, such as desalination, which is the removal of salt, and consolidation, which is the strengthening of the object’s structure.

One of the most famous examples of a major underwater archaeology project is the raising of the Mary Rose, a Tudor warship that sank in 1545. This once-thriving port was submerged by a combination of earthquakes and rising sea levels over a thousand years ago. The relationship between tourism and cultural heritage is a complex and often contentious one. While many argue that the influx of tourists poses a significant threat to the integrity of historical sites, others contend that tourism is a vital source of revenue for their preservation. This essay will discuss both perspectives before offering a concluding viewpoint.

On the one hand, it is undeniable that tourism can have a detrimental impact on cultural heritage. The sheer volume of visitors to popular sites can lead to physical degradation. For example, the ancient stone steps of Machu Picchu in Peru have been worn down by the millions of tourists who walk on them each year. Furthermore, the commercialization that often accompanies tourism can lead to the trivialization of cultural traditions. Sacred rituals may be performed as tourist spectacles, and traditional crafts may be mass-produced to cater to the souvenir market, thereby losing their authenticity and cultural significance. The infrastructure required to support mass tourism, such as hotels, restaurants, and transportation networks, can also lead to the destruction of the historical landscape.

On the other hand, tourism can be a powerful force for the preservation of cultural heritage. The revenue generated from ticket sales, souvenirs, and guided tours can be reinvested into the maintenance and restoration of historical sites. In many developing countries, tourism is the primary source of funding for heritage preservation. For instance, the revenue from tourism at Angkor Wat in Cambodia is crucial for the ongoing conservation of the temple complex. Moreover, tourism can raise awareness of the importance of cultural heritage and foster a sense of pride among local communities. When people see that their heritage is valued by others, they are more likely to take an active role in its protection.

In my opinion, while the potential threats of tourism are real, they are not insurmountable. With careful planning and management, it is possible to harness the economic benefits of tourism while minimizing its negative impacts. This can be achieved through measures such as limiting the number of visitors to sensitive sites, promoting responsible tourism practices, and ensuring that a significant portion of the revenue generated from tourism is reinvested into heritage preservation. Furthermore, it is essential to involve local communities in the planning and management of tourism, so that they can have a say in how their heritage is presented and protected.

In conclusion, the relationship between tourism and cultural heritage is a double-edged sword. While uncontrolled tourism can undoubtedly cause irreparable damage, a well-managed and sustainable tourism industry can be a powerful ally in the fight to preserve our shared human history for future generations.
